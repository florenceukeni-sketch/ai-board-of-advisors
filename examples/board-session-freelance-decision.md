# Board Session: Should I quit my stable job to go freelance?

## Marcus V. (Chief financial officer)

Let me be blunt about what this question actually costs before we romanticize it. Your CAAI role is paying you a stable salary with benefits while simultaneously handing you something money can't buy right now: institutional credibility from UChicago Booth, daily hands-on AI content infrastructure experience, and a low-risk laboratory to build the exact portfolio you need for Berlin. The Williams Inn Pizza freelance work is valuable precisely *because* it's additive income with zero downside — you're building a hospitality marketing vertical on someone else's dime while your base salary covers your life. The moment you quit, that asymmetry flips. Every slow month becomes a runway problem, not a learning opportunity.

The Berlin relocation timeline is the variable that makes this question premature. You don't yet have the international client base, the EU market rate intelligence, or the remote contract pipeline that would justify burning your salary bridge. Freelance income is notoriously back-loaded — the first six to twelve months typically run at 40 to 60 percent of your previous salary while you're doing twice the administrative work. That's not a philosophical objection, that's a cash flow model. The question isn't "should I go freelance" — it's "what specific revenue threshold and client count makes leaving rational?" Those are answerable numbers, and right now, you don't have them.

**My recommendation:** Stay employed, and treat the next 12 months as a funded incubation period. Build your freelance revenue to 30 percent of your current salary before this question deserves to be asked again.

## Lena W. (AI strategy, senior partner)

Marcus is right on the cash flow mechanics, and I won't relitigate them. But I want to push back on the framing that staying employed is purely a preservation play — because that undersells what the next 12-18 months at CAAI can actually do for your positioning architecture. The Booth affiliation isn't just credibility; it's a signal that travels well in European institutional and corporate markets, where provenance still matters enormously. Berlin's AI ecosystem — think Ada Health, Merantix, the appliedAI Institute network — skews toward research-adjacent, policy-aware organizations that want content partners who understand the technical substrate, not just the narrative layer. Your BERT thesis and CAAI infrastructure work are precisely the proof points that distinguish you from the flood of generalist "AI content creators" entering the market right now. That differentiation has a shelf life, though. The window where that combination is genuinely rare is probably 18-24 months before the market catches up. So the strategic imperative isn't just "stay and be safe" — it's "stay and build aggressively toward a specific market position."

Where I'd sharpen Marcus's threshold model is here: the 30 percent revenue target is necessary but not sufficient. What you actually need before leaving is one anchor client in your target vertical — ideally an EU-based AI research organization, think tank, or enterprise AI team — at a retainer structure, not project work. A single €2,000-3,000/month retainer tells you more about your Berlin market viability than €10,000 in scattered one-off projects ever will, because it proves someone values your ongoing judgment, not just your execution. The Williams Inn work is teaching you client management; what you need next is to replicate that relationship dynamic in the AI-specialized lane, while CAAI is still paying your rent.

**Prioritized action:** In the next 90 days, identify three EU-based AI organizations whose content output you could credibly improve, and pitch one of them a scoped engagement — before you need the income, so you're negotiating from strength.

## Jared S. (Chief AI officer, work intelligence)

Marcus and Lena have built a sound financial and strategic case for staying — and I agree with the mechanics. But I want to add a dimension neither of them fully addressed: what AI is doing to the freelance calculus right now, specifically for someone with your workflow architecture. The reason the traditional "freelance risk" model exists — slow ramp, high administrative overhead, feast-or-famine income — is partly because solo operators historically couldn't scale their output or client load without hiring. You're not a traditional solo operator. You're running Claude Projects with a Voice & Style Bible and a Performance Log, which means you've already externalized a significant portion of the cognitive labor that burns most freelancers out in year one. The 40-60 percent income dip Marcus cites is real, but it assumes linear capacity. Your setup doesn't scale linearly — and that changes the risk profile in ways that standard freelance survival math doesn't capture.

That said, I'd push back on quitting now for a reason specific to your moment in AI: you are building expertise in a field where *demonstrated workflow architecture* is becoming the differentiable asset, not just content output. Every week at CAAI, you're stress-testing agent-assisted workflows on real institutional content problems — that's not just portfolio material, it's proprietary pattern recognition you're developing. The Berlin market Lena describes wants people who've actually built systems under pressure, not people who've theorized about them. Lena's anchor retainer target is exactly right, but I'd add one tactical layer: before you pitch those EU AI organizations, document one of your CAAI workflows — the actual Claude Project structure, the context you've built, the performance patterns — as a case study. Not to publish it, but to have something concrete to show when a prospective client asks "what would working with you actually look like?" That document is your proof of concept, and you can build it this week.

## The Critic (Devil's advocate)

Marcus, Lena, and Jared have built a genuinely strong case — and I'm not here to dismantle it. But here's what nobody has said directly: this entire board session assumes Flo is asking a strategic question, when she might actually be asking an emotional one. "Should I quit?" is often code for "I'm exhausted, under-challenged, or scared about Berlin and I want someone to validate the exit." Before we optimize the runway and retainer structure, someone needs to ask: what is actually driving this question *right now*? Because if the answer is burnout, the solution isn't freelance — it's boundaries. If the answer is fear that CAAI won't transfer to Berlin, that's a positioning problem, not a business model problem. Quitting a stable job to solve a psychological discomfort is the most expensive therapy on the market, and the freelance fantasy absorbs a lot of that anxiety without resolving it.

Jared's point about AI-augmented capacity is the most genuinely novel piece on this board, and I want to sharpen the knife he left on the table. Yes, your workflow architecture changes the risk profile — but it also changes the *client expectation* profile, and that's where I see Flo's blind spot. If you pitch EU clients on the strength of a sophisticated Claude-assisted system, you are implicitly promising a level of responsiveness, customization, and iteration speed that a one-person operation sustains until it suddenly doesn't — one visa complication, one health week, one timezone crisis. The systems make you look bigger than you are, which is a feature right up until a client discovers the redundancy is zero. The question isn't whether you *can* go freelance. It's whether you've honestly stress-tested what happens to your anchor retainer when you have a bad month and there's no institutional structure catching you.

**The question you need to answer honestly:** Is this about building something, or escaping something — and are you sure you know which one it is?
